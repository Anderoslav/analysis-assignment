West Side Story Review

The endurance of a timeless classic.

By Ander Grabski
Updated: Oct 9, 2023 11:00 pm
Posted: Oct 11, 2023 10:00 pm
For over half a century, Ernest Lehman’s West Side Story (1961) has maintained a strong cultural significance in the world. This story has remained relevant to the times, and the political issues the movie brings up have remained understandable to audiences of all ages. Having seen the film only recently and planning to watch its reimagined 2021 version in the future, I can say with absolute certainty that the newer version has a lot to live up to. Lehman’s West Side Story from composer Leonard Bernstein and lyricist Stephen Sondheim effortlessly introduces the plotline with a dramatic opening sequence, and the choreography of the dancing does well in representing the personalities of those performing it. There is a saying that “You can’t improve on perfection,” and while I wouldn’t call this movie perfect, you’d be hard pressed to find parts of it that can be improved.
Based off the timeless classic of Romeo and Juliet, the story follows two teenagers connected to rival gangs in the city of New York. The two kids struggle with their emotions and aspirations as they try to find a future with a “happily ever after” type of ending. The ill intentions and violence between the two tightly knit clans makes that future unlikely. The morbidity of gang wars in the street was dialed back into an almost playful sequence of dance moves with some slightly aggressive underlying meaning behind them. But the overlying messages of inclusion, diversity, and acceptance of those who are different shine brightly like a beacon over the sea of grey that is New York City.




A notable positive of the movie is its excellent casting for the wide range of personalities present in the movie. Rita Moreno shines beautifully in her role of Anita, bringing a caring personality to what is usually a gruff tone displayed by other characters, as well as a welcome layer of diversity to the cast. George Chakiris (Bernardo) and Russ Tamblyn (Riff) portray perfect figureheads to their respective gangs by bringing a sense of confidence and ego that strengthens the partisan nature of the movie. We cannot forget about our main duo Tony and Maria, cast by Natalie Wood and Richard Beymer, respectively. Their portrayal of their characters emphasizes the emotional side of the movie that its contemporary focused on. Their actions pulled the viewer out of the gang fight mindset and back into the improbable love mindset that drives the story forward. It also helps that the back and forth between the larger picture of the Sharks and Jets and the smaller picture of Tony and Maria kept the movie exciting and prevented it from needing to spew exposition to push the next plot point.




The music has a vintage theme that corresponds perfectly to the movie’s message. Though by definition a vintage movie would have vintage aspects to it, the movie’s dated characteristics help to convey the struggles of the time to newer audiences by giving it an authentic feel. The instrumentation of the background music mixed with the fast-paced tempo blend with the lower bitrate of the sound and definition of the image to deliver a classic feel. This is part of the reason why I have doubts about its successor living up to the glory of the original. That being said, the music in the original movie is timeless and has no doubt made it into the remake. The songs work well with the broad range of tones in the cast, they’re catchy, and they resonate with the audience. I haven’t heard of most of the songs before watching, though the tunes have echoed through the generations since. This caused a peculiar phenomenon. Like putting a face to a voice, songs that I’ve been hearing my whole life were given context and background.

I can appreciate the fact that the movie has aged a considerable amount since its release, and that its age can drive people away from the movie. However, nobody can deny the importance of the classics. The story is one that’s been refined through time, the cast portrays their role with a skill that couldn’t be accomplished without them, and the music they dance to has earned a spot on the playlists of many. Overall, Lehman’s West Side Story is an excellent watch. If you’re looking for a thrilling roller coaster of emotions or are trying to spice up your seventh re-watch of Romeo and Juliet, look no further than West Side Story.

4.5 out of 5 Stars, 9/10 Score
